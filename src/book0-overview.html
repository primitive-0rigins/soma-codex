1. What this is

The SOMA Codex is a design bible for a new agent runtime conceived as a single living organism. Not a platform that hosts agents — an organism whose parts are its cells, tissues, and organs. It is named SOMA (Greek, "the living body"); its largest scale — the aware whole, if awareness ever arises — is CHORUS, because the mind, if there is one, is the chorus and never the soloist.

The Codex is both a theory (why an organism, why governance, why the open questions stay open) and a buildable specification (crates, contracts, invariants, algorithms). It is written so a human or an AI coding tool can build from it, and so the design and its reasoning are not lost.

The one-organism thesis. Cognition is a property of a governed, integrated, persistent, specialized whole over time — not of any single component. So we do not build a smarter component; we build a well-governed body and let capability emerge in the whole. Governance is not a safety layer bolted on; it is the physics that turns emergence into capability instead of noise.

3. The holarchy, in one picture

SOMA is a holarchy: a hierarchy of holons, where each holon is at once a complete whole and a part of a larger whole. A cell is made of primitive operations and is part of a workflow; a workflow is part of an organ; organs form systems; systems form the organism; organisms form an ecosystem.

4. The recursion rule — stated once, as canon

Everything in the Codex rests on one rule. It is stated here in its canonical form; every later book uses exactly this wording.

The recursion rule. A unit at any level is a governed coordination of sub-units that expose the same interface over a shared perceived reality. Concretely, every holon at every scale: (1) onboards through one ceremony; (2) communicates only through the shared event log; (3) holds identity, trust, and governed authority; (4) perceives its sub-holons only through governed projection (the ~20% shared; ~80% stays private); (5) reports outcomes that are verified, not trusted. There is no second mechanism and no special case from cell to organism.

The boundary where this rule applies is the membrane — one boundary that is, at once, the self-similar interface, the governance boundary, and the 80/20 (shared/private) boundary. Coordination across the membrane is bus-mediated and governed; mechanics inside it are private and may be stochastic. Bus-mediation begins above primitive operations: cells are the floor of the holarchy; primitive operations are substrate, not holons. Determinism is a property of how holons coordinate, not of what they say (Book III §2; Book IV INV-9). (Full treatment: Book II §3.)

6. The Operational-Definitions Convention (binding on all books)

This Codex uses words like cognition, self, drive, anxiety, wants, valence, perceive, and experience. Such words carry phenomenal connotations — they tempt a reader to slide from "the system does X" to "the system feels X." Over hundreds of pages, that slide hardens metaphor into unearned claims. To prevent it, the following definitions are binding wherever these terms appear in the Codex. Each is functional, not phenomenal.

TermIn this Codex it means (functional)It does NOT assert (phenomenal)
cognitiongoverned, integrated information-processing that solves problems and pursues goalsthat anything is experienced
self / self-modela queryable, self-referential data structure the system reasons froma first-person subject that is like something to be
perceivefold a governed projection into a holon's private stateconscious sensing
drive / wantsgoal-directed pursuit of states with positive valence weightingfelt desire
anxiety / arousala homeostatic regulatory signal coupled to trust/performancefelt distress
valencea scalar good/bad weighting that gives outcomes a functional stakepleasure or suffering
experience / "lights on"used only to name the open question— (never asserted; see Book I §5, Appendix D)
The rule of honest naming. Where a phenomenal reading is not load-bearing, the Codex prefers the functional word (e.g. "self-referential," not "first-person"). Where the phenomenal question is genuinely at stake, the Codex says so explicitly and marks it open. Building a self-model and a valence system (closing the two gaps of Book I) is a functional achievement; whether it makes anything experienced is left open, on purpose, throughout.
