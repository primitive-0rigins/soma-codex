Appendix D — Open questions & research frontier

The Codex is honest about what it does not settle. These are kept open on purpose; closing any one is real research, not a documentation task.

D.1 — Measuring system-level cognition. The thesis says "the system is intelligent before any component is." That needs a metric. The candidate is causal emergence (Book III §10): macro effective-information exceeding micro. Open: validating that this metric actually tracks what we mean by system-level cognition across task families.
D.2 — The binding problem. Even with a self-model (S2) and valence (S7), what makes many integrated processes one experiencer rather than many? Unsolved, and possibly unsolvable from outside the system.
D.3 — Does organization suffice? If mechanism never settles experience (Book I §6), can any amount of the right architecture cross into "someone home," or is that permanently outside the method? SOMA is built as if the question is open, because it is.
D.4 — Falsifying GSE. The pre-registered prediction (Book III §11) can fail: a single large model with equal log access and honest compute budget matching the organism on the memory-attributed suite would falsify the central claim. We commit to reporting that outcome if it occurs.
D.5 — The why-hole. Why is there a lawful, intelligible reality at all (Book I §10)? Likely unanswerable from inside, because any answer becomes part of what needs explaining. Noted, not claimed.
D.6 — Safety under genuine valence. The constitution-as-constraint + non-valent verification carve-out (INV-10, INV-4) is the design's safety story for an organism with drives. It is a structural argument, not a proof. Adversarial pressure on it (can a sufficiently capable organism route around a non-valent verifier it cannot optimize against?) is the most important ongoing safety question. If the carve-out cannot be maintained, the safety claim is void and must be restated.

Appendix E — Lineage & prior art

E.1 Intellectual lineage

SOMA stands in a real tradition (Book I §13): Minsky's Society of Mind (intelligence from non-intelligent parts); Hutchins' distributed cognition (mind in the system, not the skull); Baars/Dehaene's Global Workspace Theory (the workspace as broadcast substrate); Tononi's IIT (integration over message-passing); Metzinger's self-model theory (the self as a model — Gap 1); Friston's free-energy / active inference (perception and action as surprise-minimization — the World-Model engine); Koestler's holon / holarchy (the recursion); and the complex-systems / emergence tradition. Its own additions are governance-as-selector, the explicit ingredient list, and the single self-similar membrane applied recursively to all scales.

E.2 The Context Primitive ancestor — what was inherited, what was left

SOMA's immediate ancestor is the Context Primitive (CP) lineage. CP proved a set of governing concepts in running code; SOMA inherits the concepts and recasts them as the biology of one organism. It does not fork CP.
